Speedcubing Guides & Tutorials

In-depth guides to help you transition from intermediate CFOP solver to sub-15 expert.

F2L Technique

1. Breaking the 20-Second Barrier: F2L Efficiency

For most cubers, the First Two Layers (F2L) is the most time-consuming stage of the solve. Moving past basic inserts requires shifting from mechanical formula-solving to intuitive block-building.

Ditch the Rotations

A single cube rotation (y or y') takes about 0.3 to 0.5 seconds. If you rotate 4 or 5 times during F2L, you are wasting up to 2.5 seconds just spinning the cube. Aim for a maximum of 1 or 2 rotations per F2L stage by utilizing the back slots and learning rear slot insertions.

Keyhole Method

When you have a case where either the corner is already solved in the bottom slot (but the edge is missing), or the edge is already solved (but the corner is missing), do not take the solved piece out. Instead, temporarily rotate the bottom layer (D or D') to align an empty slot under the target position, slide the matching piece in, and restore the bottom layer. This reduces move count significantly.

Last Layer

2. Advanced Last Layer: 2-Side PLL Recognition

Waiting to see 3 sides of the cube before executing your PLL algorithm wastes critical inspection time. Learning 2-Side PLL recognition allows you to identify your case instantly from any angle.

Headlights and Blocks

Every PLL case has unique relationships between colors on adjacent faces. The key features to look for are:

  • Headlights: Two matching corner stickers on the same face, separated by a different color (e.g., Red-Green-Red).
  • Blocks: Two or more matching adjacent stickers (corner and edge) forming a solid bar of color.
  • Opposite Colors: Stickers on the same face that belong to opposite sides of the cube (e.g., Orange next to Red).

How to Practice

Load up the 2-Side PLL Recognition trainer tab on our homepage. Study the 3D visual representations and practice identifying cases based on the headlights and bars. With practice, recognition becomes subconscious.

Lookahead

3. The Secret to Flow: Lookahead and TPS Control

Many intermediate cubers focus too much on TPS (Turns Per Second). However, spinning the cube at 10 TPS only to pause for 2 seconds searching for the next piece is slower than turning at a constant 4 TPS with zero pauses.

Slow Down to Speed Up

Practice turning at a slow, deliberate pace where you can solve your current F2L pair without looking at it. While your hands execute the active pair using muscle memory, your eyes should actively search the rest of the cube for the next corner and edge pieces.

Track the Pieces

When inserting a pair, pay attention to where other pieces move. For example, if you see an edge in the top layer, watch how it shifts when you do your insertion. This allows you to plan your next moves before the current ones are even completed.

Advanced CFOP

4. Planning the First Slot: Cross + 1

The transition between finishing your cross and starting your first F2L pair is the biggest bottleneck for solvers. Elite cubers eliminate this pause by planning their first F2L pair during the 15-second inspection time.

How to Plan Cross + 1

Don't just look for your cross edges during inspection. Once you find them and plan your cross moves, locate the corner and edge pieces for your easiest F2L pair. Mentally track how those two pieces will move as you execute your cross. When your cross is done, you should already know exactly where the first F2L pair is and how to insert it.
